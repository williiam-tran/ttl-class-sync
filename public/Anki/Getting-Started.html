Tips for Creating Good Anki Flashcards

1 ‚Äì Learn FIRST, and THEN create flashcards from what you‚Äôve learned

Just like what I said a while ago, you gotta learn first before you make your cards.

If you‚Äôre just starting out, all you need to know is that the purpose of these flashcards is to test what you already learned.

Folks, it‚Äôs ‚Äúremember what you learn‚Äú, and not ‚Äúlearn what you remember‚Äú.

It‚Äôs easy to get confident that you can remember anything using Anki, but the truth is that NONE of that matters if you do not understand the material you‚Äôre putting in.

(Unless you‚Äôre really memorizing isolated facts, but even so, context matters for retention)

Well, what‚Äôs the worst that could happen when you don‚Äôt follow this rule?

You‚Äôll know how to answer the flashcard but you‚Äôll find out that you cannot apply the ‚Äúknowledge‚Äù anywhere else.

In other words, you just get pseudoknowledge.

2 ‚Äì One simple card is worth 2x the vague cards

Don‚Äôt try to put in paragraphs in a card.

Don‚Äôt even try to put ‚ÄúExplain‚Äù type of questions.

Break them down until it‚Äôs simple FOR YOU.

This brings me to my next point.

3 ‚Äì The number of cards don‚Äôt matter much; coverage matters more

There‚Äôs no optimal number of cards.

Think about what‚Äôs important here: It‚Äôs important that you have made cards that can cover the entire material you‚Äôve understood.

So when adding cards, it doesn‚Äôt matter if you have plenty of cards just by studying a chapter as long as you follow the two rules above.

What matters is you actually learn the concepts thoroughly.

Break a concept as much as possible.

By doing these two things, you‚Äôll feel like you‚Äôre able to recall each card in less than a second. (Well, not physically less than a second, but that‚Äôs how it‚Äôll feel like üòâ)

It‚Äôs much, much faster compared to creating a few but long, complicated cards.

For example, instead of:

‚ÄúWhat are Newton‚Äôs Three Laws of Motion?‚Äù

You write questions like:

  • ‚ÄúWhat is Newton‚Äôs First Law of Motion?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúWhat is Newton‚Äôs Third Law of Motion?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúWhich law states F=ma?‚Äù (obviously, this question covers one law in another angle ‚Äî also called redundancy)

This reduces ambiguity ‚Äî making your cards faster to answer. Compare that to doing a mini-brain dump for each card and you‚Äôll realize it gets tiresome pretty quickly.

Just to be clear, there‚Äôs no stopping you from creating both types of cards.

Feel free to experiment, because there really are times when a longer card can be more efficient. Let‚Äôs avoid black-and-white thinking here.

Smash That Spacebar!

Studying using Anki is pretty straightforward.

You just open the app, click a deck with due cards, and you‚Äôre set.

When a card shows up, you just press on the spacebar to show the answer.

When the answer shows up, you are given choices below to choose from: Again, Good, Easy.

Using Anki default settings, Anki will show the card again after a certain amount depending on how difficult it was for you to recall the card.

  • Again ‚Äì Less than a minute, the card will show up again
  • Good ‚Äì The card will show up in less than 10 minutes
  • Easy ‚Äì The card will show up after 4 days

My method is simple:

  • You press Again when you failed to recall the answer,
  • You press Good when you successfully recall the answer
  • You press Easy when you meet two things: you already know the answer a long time ago, and you have recalled the answer in an instant
